Why the name

SLICC is a Self-Licking Ice Cream Cone — a recursive browser agent that can extend and operate itself. The vocabulary is not decoration. It is the architecture.

The cast

Cone

The main agent — one per session. Orchestrates, delegates, synthesizes, and gates external handoffs. Not a side agent; the boss scoop with isCone: true.

Cone & Scoops guide

Scoops

Side agents with isolated sandboxes and conversations. Parallel workers the cone feeds via scoop_scoop and feed_scoop. Heavy lifting happens here.

Cone & Scoops guide

Licks

External interrupts — not agents. Webhooks, cron, navigate handoffs, sprinkle clicks, file watches. They wake the cone (or a targeted scoop) for a turn.

Licks & Automation guide

The juxtaposition

The illustration stacks three crafts that rarely share a frame:

  • Disney cel warmth — the cone and scoops as characters you can trust
  • Pinocchio mechanics — wooden joints and a conductor's baton; delegation made visible
  • Art-deco Chicago steel — licks as rivets racing rails; async events with industrial click-clank, not chat bubbles

Soft gelato recursion meets hard async machinery. Light on the self-licking loop (the recursive subject). Shadow on the rivet rails (the interrupt layer). The orchestrator in the middle holding the composition together — same layout as the system diagram, told as lore instead of Mermaid.

One line to remember

Cone = orchestrator · Scoops = side agents · Licks = external pings that wake one of them.

Licks are doorbells. Scoops are who answers when the cone sends them. The cone is who you talk to — and who must approve a navigate handoff before anything runs.

Handoffs across agents

When Grok, Claude, or another terminal agent hands work to SLICC, it arrives as a navigate lick: an RFC 8288 Link header or a POST /api/handoff to the local node-server. The cone shows an approval card. Accept dispatches by verb — handoff: to continue the task, upskill: to install a skill from GitHub.
